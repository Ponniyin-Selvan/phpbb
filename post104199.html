INDIA: Open Letter: Why Should Dalits Embrace Hindutva?


J. B. Rajeshwar

(Ed. note: This open letter was written to K. S. Sudershan, chief sankalchak (state or regional leader) of the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS), or National Volunteer Corps, and Sonia Gandhi, the leader of the Congress Party, and other members of the People Integration Council. The letter was written in response to Sudershan's call to people of other faiths in India to "return home" to Hinduism. The title was added by the editor.)

Dear Mr. Sudershan,

Of late, you and your bandwagon of associates have been asking all other religious citizens of India to convert to Hinduism so that all of us can have a unified identity called Hindutva instead of being called an Indian citizen by virtue of birth within the boundary of this country. You have also been asserting that there is nothing about this Hindutva identity of which one should be ashamed. I would be grateful if you can clarify my doubts after reading the following history of my people, who had the audacity to liberate themselves from the clutches of the Aryan-imposed caste system on the people of this land.



"Why is it that we, the majority Dravidian mass of this land, should be subservient to your minority Aryan population?" a Dalit asks K. S. Sudershan, a leader of the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS), in India, the world's largest democracy. (Photo: Tiane Doan de Champassak, UNESCO)


The higher caste rulers of our area, when they visited our dwellings, pointed out that the women had to go to their abodes for their enjoyment. It so happened that in one place, after pointing out the women they needed for that day's enjoyment, they found out on returning home that the women of their choice were not sent to their abode for their enjoyment. The people of that locality had decided to disobey the dictate of the higher caste. This angered the rulers, and they invoked the Manu Sasthra and its associate scriptures and meted out severe punishments in the following ways using the following principles:

For Brahmins (Aryans): A Brahmin is the shape of dharma. The Brahmin who explains dharma reaches the glory of knowledge. . . . The Brahmin has been created as the first of all the creation to safeguard and execute the socio-religio ethical laws imposed on man. . . . The Brahmin is the first-born and of higher birth. Because of this privilege, a Brahmin is eligible to claim all wealth created by Brahma as his. (Manu, introduction) If a Brahmin gets a buried treasure, all of it will belong to him. Because a Brahmin is of higher stature, it should be taken into consideration that such treasures had been gathered of yore by self; and if a king gets a treasure, he should keep half of it and distribute the rest to the Brahmins. (Manu 8:100) Even if a Brahmin is doing menial work, he should be worshiped. (Prasara Smirthi 8:33)

For Shudrans (Dravidians): Lead should be boiled and poured into the two ears of the Shudran who listens to the scriptures. A Shudran is equal to a burning ghat, and thus, scripture recital should not be done near him. (Brahmasuthra 1-3-38) The tongue of a Shudran who utters the scriptures should be cut and the scriptures taken away from him and the chest that confirmed these scriptures should be split. (Thirumananda Kosambi, Lord Buddha) Without asking him, with force and without hesitation, the house of a Shudran can be looted if he is not content with carrying dirt and has acquired wealth in his house. (Manu, introduction) In whichever nation a Shudran is doing the work of dharma inquiry (justice), the work that should be done by the king of that nation, then the whole nation, as it watches, suffers like a cow caught in a quagmire. (Manu 8:21) A Shudran should not be worshiped even if he is the embodiment of good character. (Prasara Smirthi 8:33)

On the strength of the above, we received our punishment as follows:

Our forefathers were treated worse than slaves.

Our forefathers, both men and women, were forced to wear a long piece of cloth covering only the portion of their bodies from the hips to the knees, exposing all the other body parts for the benefit of the higher castes; called the Nada caste, they were placed at the bottom rung of the caste hierarchy.

Our women were forbidden by law to wear upper garments, or blouses, before the reputed upper castes.

Boys reaching the age of maturity had to pay a beard and moustache tax.

Girls reaching the age of maturity had to pay a breast tax. The tax collectors will come and measure the upcoming breast and tax accordingly. It is recorded in history that a girl cut off one of her breasts and gave it to the tax collector as a gift as she could not put up with the molestation of the tax collector.

We could not own any cattle or chickens; and if upper castes learned of it, they would be confiscated through the rule of law.

We were not allowed to educate ourselves.

Our women were abused as much as possible, which can be inferred from the following saying: It is sin to look at the modesty of the cow whereas it is the birthright of the upper caste to gaze at the naked modesty of the lower caste women and enjoy.

Any lower caste looking at the chastity of the cow should be thrashed with neem sticks, and any lower caste women found wearing an upper cloth should be thrashed with coconut frond sticks.

It was considered an insult to the upper caste women if the lower caste women were to wear an upper cloth, which is the traditional dress of the upper caste women.

We were people who were untouchable only as far as the caste system was concerned. The upper castes could not eat or drink from the same plate or tumbler that we use. We were treated with inhumanity. But when it came to raping or touching or enjoying our women, by some miracle, the bug of untouchability did not defile the upper castes, which otherwise would be defiled even if an Untouchable stood very close to them. The coming of Christian missionaries and their revulsion to seeing bare-breasted women wandering around them almost naked began the process of our transformation.

It took 37 years of struggle for us from May 1822 to July 26, 1859, to break out of this exploitation in a revolution called the Upper Cloth Revolts, supported by Christian missionaries and finally government legislation to free them of this indecent life.

It is a recorded fact that foreigners did not donate one single penny towards the cost of our freedom struggle but only gave us advice and moral support.

Christianity gave us relief from virtual slavery to the Aryan upper caste Nairs who were exploiting us.

Christianity gave us education.

Christianity gave us respectability, and we became the Nadar people.

The liberation attained by the Nada people who became Christians was extended to those sections of society that did not convert to Christianity through another law. This is why we have Hindu and Christian Nadar communities today who are well off and well respected and considered as higher caste people.

Of course, the sad part of our history is that in central Tamil Nadu, even until now, a part of our community is treated with disrespect because of their job as palmyra tree climbers. They are not allowed entry into the main temples. The chest of a palmyra climber will be full of bruises due to the climbing of the trees bare-chested. Consequently, everyone of this area is required to enter the temples bare-chested in order to identify the people who should not be entering the temple.

My questions to Mr. Sudershan and his brigade of Hindu nationalists are:

Where were our cultural champions of today's India then?

Will Hindu cultural nationalism mean that we have to go back to our old enslaved cultural ways?

Since in my present religion I do not have any caste, once you convert me back to Hinduism, will you provide a casteless place?

What is Hindutva in reality?

Why is it important that only Aryan Brahmins should remain at the top of the caste ladder?

Why is it that we, the majority Dravidian mass of this land, should be subservient to your minority Aryan population?

Why cannot the energy you and your brigade unleash on other religious communities be channelled toward the abolition of the caste system and the peaceful coexistence of all human beings?

I have many more questions to ask. If you can give me and my community clear and satisfactory answers, I, along with my bandwagon, are ready to convert into a Hindu to make your goals a success without bloodshed.

Dear Mrs. Gandhi and others of the People Integration Council,

My questions to you are:

What is the intention of starting this forum?

Will this integration succeed in ending the caste system because, without ending the caste system, integration is not possible?

Is this "People's Integration Council" being created only for political purposes for gathering votes?

You have said that we should be proud of our past cultural history. After reading the above, do you think that I can be proud of my background?

I would be grateful to receive honest answers on behalf of the people for whom you have started this forum.

J. B. Rajeshwar

Hyderabad
