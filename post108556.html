Hi,

To all those who are enrolled for the mallai trip - will take this as
a sneak peak - preview...below informations thanks to Sri Swaminathan
sir) - i wasnt joking when i said he knows every chisel mark in
mallai..


The shore temple is actually three shrines bundled into one...the
central ( some say older) sthala sayana perumal - he who sleeps on the
ground - if you notice there is no sesha ( the new temple inside town
which is now called stahala sayana perumal - actually has sesha - so
its sesha sayana )....Rajasimha pallava erected two shiva shrines
around this shrine.... This eastern temple is called
Kshatriyasimhesvara, and the western one is Rajasimhesvara. He also
called the Vishnu shrine Narapati-simha-Pallava-Vishnu-griha. These
names are inscribed on the temples and are named after the titles of
Rajasimha. The inscription of Rajaraja, the greatest Chola, found here
calls the complex as Jalasayanam and the Vishnu shrine as Pall-
kandaruliya-thevar

Varaha in animal form

Let us look at the boar. The boar is most likely a representation of
Varaha avatara. This is a very realistic depiction.
We can be certain about this, as the boar's snout is rooting downward
as in mythology. Also whorls and lotus shown between the legs should
represent ocean. Its being carved in the round is also important, as
the Pallava-s didn't sculpt many sculptures in the round. The three
free-standing animals in the Ratha Complex and the monkey family near
the Penance panel are the only other sculptures of this genre.
Its importance is even beyond the quality of execution. Firstly, it is
made in the live-rock. This could mean that it is a monolith too.

There are also three important inscriptions on the front of the
pedestal, which are titles of Rajasimha. The language is Sanskrit and
the Pallava Grantha is the script.

The titles inscribed are: in the front are Sri Rajasimhah (`lion-among-
kings'), Sri Ranajayah (`victorious-in-battles') and Sribharah
(`upholder-of-prosperity'), and one, Sri Chitrakarmmukah (`wonderful-
archer') on the western flank. That the same titles in the same order
appear in Mamallapuram, Kanchi Kailasanatha Temple and Talagirisvara
Temple in Panamalai, add to their importance.

Talking of inscriptions, we have another important close by. Very
close to the boar, on the inner faces of three upper rim stone slabs
on the south-west side of the well we have the following lines:
1st line on the top step:
. . . rAjasimho yaH kSatrasimha iti vishruta-puNya-kIrrtiH jIyA-
2nd line on the loer step:
t-[ma]hesvara-sikhAmaNi-dIpta-mauli-
3rd line on the lowers step:
[ryyu]ddhArjjuno nikhila-loka-nAre

This can be translated as: "Â… Rajasimha, whose well-merited fame is
widespread as Lion among Warriors, whose crown's crest-jewel's
splendor is Mahesvara, who is the 'Arjuna-of-War', long live
that 'Lion of a King among Men, Ruler of the entire world."

There is another inscription of Rajasimha, on a pillar in Vayalur,
which contains exactly the above text.
There is something more. There is a possibility that this image has
suffered vandalism. Again, my God! The sculpture looks reassembled.
During the excavation unearthing this complex this image was found in
pieces; these pieces were put together. One may suspect that it was
deliberate vandalism, as you can see even now a number of wedge holes.
But all the self-praise and after causing a number of shrines for
Siva, why did Rajasimha create this unique image of an avatara of
Vishnu in this complex? You can add this to the other Pallava quizzes
in your bag.
