by aprilfool_75 on Tue Sep 09, 2008 7:45 pm

I am branching out this thread "Tribute to Kunnakudi" to the subject
mentioned in the subject line.

As mentioned healthy competition gave us good songs I am adding a few more
examples.


1. No one can forget the song "Vaan Nila Nila Alla" song. This was Mr.MSV
and Kaviarasar Combo. Mr.MSV had decided on the tune and asked Kaviarasar to
come out with lyrics which would end with 'La'. Earlier Kaviarasar was
lethargic or I am not sure what mind set he was in ... he didnt produce his
gems. Mr.MSV wanted the best of Kaviarasar and started scolding him. Why are
you called Kaviarasar when you cant write a simple lyric for such a simple
tune. Kaviarasar got furious and wrote about 15 saranams for that song.
2. Ninaithaale Inikkum is a KB movie and Sujtha was in charge of story
and Screen play. This particular incident Sujatha had narrated in his
Katradhum Petradhum book. KB once asked Sujatha, whether he had attended any
song composition session of MSV and Kaviarasar, for which Sujatha answered
in negative. KB took him to the studio where MSV was present with his
harmonium. Kaviarasar was asusual late. For the first 15 - 20 minutes they
both were discussing about health (Kusalam Visarichu aachu!) Then MSV
reminded him about the song composition and proposed the tune. Thannane
Thannane Thannane Thannane ... Immediate was Kaviarasar's reply "Yengeyum
Yeppodum Sangeetham Sandhosham" MSV sang the tune for the next line.
Kaviarasar enquired KB whether he can say something philasophical and KB
agreed. Immediate was Kaviarasar's lyric "Rathirigal Vandhuvittal
Sastirangal Odividum" Sujatha has written that the whole song was completed
in 15 minutes. Sujatha asked Kaviarasar, "How is it possible for him to
write lyrics with such an ease". Kaviarasar's answer was simple, "If you
know the "Sandha Varthaigal" then it is not at all difficult" -- Easier said
than done.

I can keep on writing about Kaviarasar but working on a project proposal and
my Group head is sitting on my head ...

Laters!

trivia about IR


1. He composed songs for Kadhal Oviyum (Bharathiraja / IR/ Vairamuthu in
which Radh and a blind man play the lead role) in two days and that too due
to non availability of SPB. All the songs in the movie are super duper hit.
2. Who can forget "Ilamai yenum Poongadru" song (This song is for Sridevi
and the actor who plays Radhika's fathers role in the latest serial) from
the film "Pagalil oru Iravu" After the song lyrics was handed over to IR he
composed the tunes and the recorded the song in 17 minutes!
3. In the film "Sindhu Bhairavi" when ever Suhasini is in scene the
background score is based on Sindhu Bhairavi Raham. He will display all the
facets of that Raga in that film. All IR fans would have noticed that.
4. Mari Mari Ninne song in Sindhu Bhairavi. The original raham by Saint
Tyagaraja for this song was Kambodhi. But this man had the audacity to
compose the song based on Saramathi. If I have to explain in cricketing
terms - Play a cover drive on a over pitched ball. Hook a ball when it is
short pitched and raising. This is the cricketing grammar. But this man
composing Mari Mari Ninne in Saramathi is like hooking a over pitched
delivery to the square leg boundary for a clean six!
5. Experimenting with Ragas like composing a song on a raga which is
supposed to depict Sad mood. "Kandu Pudichen Kandhu Pudichen" from Guru
Sishyan. This is based on Bhavapriya Raga and this raga is full of sorrow
mood.
6. Composing songs on rare ragas which others dare to touch like
"Simhendra Madhyamam", "Kanakangi" (This is the 1st raga in the Melakartha),
"Natakapriya", "Ramapriya", "Reethigowlai", "Rasikapriya"(72nd Melakarta
Raga)

by sillybuffer on Wed Sep 10, 2008 9:32 am

Aah Raja the Great... it is truly wonderful to listen to his creations and
one can appreciate it at so many different levels.. carnatic classical or
western.. here is something I had written on a Guitar forum on Madai
Thiranthu.. this might not be everyone's cup of tea but just to give you a
flavor for how we can all appreciate Raja in our own way:

Anyway, this is a post I made to the guitar forum and discusses the tamil
song Madai Thiranthu and its chord construction/progression etc. I doubt it
will be of too much interest to people other than those who follow tamil
songs AND play some instrument..

I thought that I would start off by posting the chords to the song "Madai
Thiranthu" from Nizhalgal..

I play the song in the A#maj Scale. I don't know if this is the original one
since I worked out the chords for this a few years ago, but it still works.
If this is not the original scale, you can always move it around.

Ok.. the A#maj scale consists of the following notes:

A# C D D# F G A

The corresponding chords are:

A#, Cm, Dm, D#, F, Gm and Am-dim

(I apologize for being a little pedantic here but I have found that it
usually helps to understand the scale first before going on to the chords).

The other thing to note is that you could call this the A#maj Scale or the
Gmin Scale. The Gmin is the relative minor of the A#maj scale. (On the
fretboard, you can identify this by going down three semitones (half notes)
to find the relative minor of a major scale). The notes and chords remain
the same except that you would start from G instead of A#. On second
thoughts, seeing how the song shapes up, I would rather call this the Gmin
Scale.

Anyway, let me give the chords first before a brief discussion:


Dm Gm A# Dm
Madai thiRanthu, Thaavum nathi alai naan,

Dm Gm A# Dm
Manam thiRanthu, koovum thiru kuyil naan,

Dm Cm Gm
Isai Kalaingan, yen aasaigaL aayiram

Gm A# Dm Cm F
Ninaiththathu PaLiththathu

Intro - 1
---------

Stanza - 1
==========

Dm A# Gm A#
Kaalam KaNinthathu, kathavugaL thiRanthathu

Dm A# Gm A#
Nyaanam ViLainthathu, nal Isai piRanthathu

Cm Cm Cm A#
Puthu raagam padaippathaale, Naanum iRaivanE,

A# A# A# Cm
Isaikkena IsaigindRa rasigargaL raajjiyam,

Cm Dm
YenakkE thaan.



The second stanza is similar.

A few things to note:

In the Gmin Scale, the chords are so:


Gm Amaj-dim A# Cm Dm D# F

I IV V


where I, IV and V denote the tonic, subdominant and dominant of the scale.

1. The piece starts off with the V (Dm) and then resolves to the tonic (Gm)
at the very beginning (Madai Thiranthu). I find this interesting because
usually, people start off a song with the tonic and usually follow some
chord progression, resoving it to the tonic again towards the end.


2. The chord progression in the first lines is I - III - V (nice.. different
from the usual I-IV-V.

3. I like the resolution to tonic at the end of the (Ninaiththathu
paLiththathu).. as Gm - A# - Dm - Cm - F which follows a I-III-V-IV-VII
resolving to I at the next stanza.

4. The stanzas have the same basic I-III-V progression before getting to
IV-V at the end (Cm - Dm) .. and letting it hang like that. Creates a
certain tension that can ONLY be released by resolving to the I, which is
done at the next "Madai Thiranthu" when the Gm is played. I have found that
I usually love songs that do this uniformally.. (also songs where there are
scale changes... but thats for another day).

5. In the second interlude, Raja subtly adds am F# into the mix. Now this is

Anyway, this is a post I made to the guitar forum and discusses the
tamil
song Madai Thiranthu and its chord construction/progression etc. I
doubt it
will be of too much interest to people other than those who follow
tamil
songs AND play some instrument..

I thought that I would start off by posting the chords to the
song "Madai
Thiranthu" from Nizhalgal..

I play the song in the A#maj Scale. I don't know if this is the
original one
since I worked out the chords for this a few years ago, but it
still works.
If this is not the original scale, you can always move it around.

Ok.. the A#maj scale consists of the following notes:

A# C D D# F G A

The corresponding chords are:

A#, Cm, Dm, D#, F, Gm and Am-dim

(I apologize for being a little pedantic here but I have found that
it
usually helps to understand the scale first before going on to the
chords).

The other thing to note is that you could call this the A#maj Scale
or the
Gmin Scale. The Gmin is the relative minor of the A#maj scale. (On
the
fretboard, you can identify this by going down three semitones
(half notes)
to find the relative minor of a major scale). The notes and chords
remain
the same except that you would start from G instead of A#. On second
thoughts, seeing how the song shapes up, I would rather call this
the Gmin
Scale.

Anyway, let me give the chords first before a brief discussion:


Dm Gm A# Dm
Madai thiRanthu, Thaavum nathi alai naan,

Dm Gm A# Dm
Manam thiRanthu, koovum thiru kuyil naan,

Dm Cm Gm
Isai Kalaingan, yen aasaigaL aayiram

Gm A# Dm Cm F
Ninaiththathu PaLiththathu

Intro - 1
---------

Stanza - 1
==========

Dm A# Gm A#
Kaalam KaNinthathu, kathavugaL thiRanthathu

Dm A# Gm A#
Nyaanam ViLainthathu, nal Isai piRanthathu

Cm Cm Cm A#
Puthu raagam padaippathaale, Naanum iRaivanE,

A# A# A# Cm
Isaikkena IsaigindRa rasigargaL raajjiyam,

Cm Dm
YenakkE thaan.



The second stanza is similar.

A few things to note:

In the Gmin Scale, the chords are so:


Gm Amaj-dim A# Cm Dm D# F

I IV V


where I, IV and V denote the tonic, subdominant and dominant of the
scale.

1. The piece starts off with the V (Dm) and then resolves to the
tonic (Gm)
at the very beginning (Madai Thiranthu). I find this interesting
because
usually, people start off a song with the tonic and usually follow
some
chord progression, resoving it to the tonic again towards the end.


2. The chord progression in the first lines is I - III - V (nice..
different
from the usual I-IV-V.

3. I like the resolution to tonic at the end of the (Ninaiththathu
paLiththathu).. as Gm - A# - Dm - Cm - F which follows a I-III-V-IV-
VII
resolving to I at the next stanza.

4. The stanzas have the same basic I-III-V progression before
getting to
IV-V at the end (Cm - Dm) .. and letting it hang like that. Creates
a
certain tension that can ONLY be released by resolving to the I,
which is
done at the next "Madai Thiranthu" when the Gm is played. I have
found that
I usually love songs that do this uniformally.. (also songs where
there are
scale changes... but thats for another day).

5. In the second interlude, Raja subtly adds am F# into the mix.
Now this is
obviously outside the scale but nobody bothered to tell the Maestro
that
It creates a very nice effect.
