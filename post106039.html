Hello, am wondering if anyone has read the original book by
Kothamangalam Subbu. Sadly it is out of print but old ananda vikatan
bound volumes may be available in some people's homes. Lot of people
do not even know this classic was a book, story serialised in AV, and
with really nice pictures by Gopulu. It was very difficult to treat
with complex descriptions of intricacies of music and dance, I got
through two chapters with great difficulty with my grandmother's aid.
Would love to read it again but unfortunately I don't think it is
available.

The movie was also good but very commerialised version of the story
with much older actors.

If anyone knows more would be glad to know!!

dear venkat

i would differ on that, even though the concept was god send - i
think the whole curriculum is ...... more with an aim to cram and
pass the exam - which unfortunately was ( is) how students see the
exam. it is refreshing for eg, when we see how the curriculum for
the local schools in singapore is - its more hands on, instead of
read / memorise - the focus is on fundamentals and understanding.
frankly tamil was a more dreaded subject to me than even maths. it
was a chore even to read the text let alone look outside...so at
that time the notes were best - mug /vomit...fill it shut it forget
it. ( the best part was i still remember - they were passed on from
hand to hand - so cant even call it second hand...and i still
remember mine was a full 8 year old book.....) nothing changed...the
questions remained the same ( maybe the guys setting the papers
refered to the notes as well)....so easiest was to skip ( sleep) all
tamil class - take last 5 years paper and you could work out 10
questions and ( no need to even catch the ant....) - you had almost
80% of the possible question paper on hand. i was a past expert at
this form of divining...

while on the subject, my english teacher was smart - she would
prepare us for the larger goals at hand - her monthly test were very
simple - 2 questions - 50 marks each....simple questions but aimed
at bringing out the best in you in form of command over the
language...she used to push us to read a lot of other texts/novels (
hardy boys, secret seven, famous five, nancy drew included)...

it stood us good steed, when we worked on professional exams -
infact our proffessors would encourage open book exams - believe me
they are the toughest...

that why now in hindsight, i blame the system for not instigating a
love for the language...which could have been so easy for something
as divine as tamil.....instead the notes, gave an easy escape route.
we won the war but lost the battle..

Hi,

Thillana Mohanambal was available till recently and I have a copy at home from the last edition of Palaniappa Bros. It's one of my all-time favouritesmainly because of the way Tamil and "isai" are blended in Kalaimani's writing. I don't think any novel other than Sivakamiyin Selvan and Thillana Mohanambal has done more for increasing the layman's interest in the traditional art of Bharata Natyam. TM goes one step further and has eulogised nadaswaram, which has since been severely neglected in Tamil Nadu.

Hi venkat,

Thats the whole point.. passing an exam that is structured to test
your ability to memorise rather than understanding of the
language..so instead of leaving lasting impression on you it leaves
a lasting scar....leave alone avid writers like you - how many of us
can write a para in tamil - not talking of spellings here ( never
got the rendu suzhi, moonu suzhi right)... by a para i mean can they
write abstract / not even verse something of their own, be it just
their thoughts, comments - is there any free thought that occurs
amongst your millions

... take a look at our colleges - hardly anyone inside ( talking of
the BA, Bcom, BSc stuff) - why because it doesnt make any sense to
be inside - to pass and get a first class - all you need is a
dedicated week of cramming.

this coupled with the fact that in most english medium schools (
incl mine) students are caned if they are caught conversing in
tamil...

parents - know a lot of them who consiously talk in english ( the
nuni nakku variety) to get their kids to ape....

so the brains are being consiously programmed to think in english -
and what little tamil comes out in written form is actually thought
of in english and then translated ( sometimes using madras univ
lexicons.....) into tamil.

what is thought in our schools and colleges as tamil is just a
miniscule percentage of what is there ( maybe a few kurals, a few
poems), from that only a minute miniscule is tested, of that a
further fraction is retained.

have nothing wrong in developing english skills - they are survival
skills - but that neednt come at the expense of your mother
tongue..especially one that is so classy likes ours.
