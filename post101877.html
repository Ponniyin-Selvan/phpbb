The fall of Chenla

---------------------------------------------------------------------
-----------

Project: The fall of Chenla
Author: Lem Chuck Moth

Started date: June/01/2003
Last updated: July/01/2007
All right reserved.
Note:
Since this paper is still drafted, the readers would be advised to
ignore any context errors. The content is not final and subjected to
be reviewed. Please kindly notify me of the discrepancies.


---------------------------------------------------------------------
-----------



INTRODUCTION
The assault of Chenla, mighty as it was, did no obviously wipe out
the Funan Empire entirely from the mainland of Indochina. As soon as
they repositioned themselves, the Funan rulers were engaging in
harassing the Chenla Empire and not before long they were able to
control the northern part of mainland Indochina. In an effort to
ease the tension with China, the Funan rulers sent diplomatic
embassies to the Chinese court.
In the periods of Won-to (618-626) and (627-649), says the history
of the Tang, the Funan resent theirs embassy to China court bringing
as tribute two men of white heads.
These historical facts agree with the Khmer tradition that during
the surprise attack of the Cham kings, the Thong Family took refuge
at Nokor Rajasima.
Taking by surprise, Prah bat Devavamsa escaped north to nokor
rajasima, in the territory of the great Kamboja.
As Bhavavarman had his mind changed about Buddhism and married the
princess Kambojarajalaksmi of the original Funan line, his political
standing about the Funan Empire was changed. Undoubtedly, Dvaravati
under a direct descendant of the royal couple was aligning itself
with the displaced Funan kings to fight against Isanavarman.
THE EMERGENCE OF SRI VIJAYA
The Chinese monk I-ching on his pilgrimage to India stopped at South
East Asia and wrote at the end of the seventh century. In former
Funan the law of the Buddha prospered and spread, but a wicked king
has completely destroyed it and there are no more monks. The event
was undoubtedly related to the assault of Chenla over the Funan
Empire and the "wicked king" was no other than the Chenla king
Bhavavarman or his brother Citrasena. His coronation name
Bhavavarman, a protÃ©gÃ© of Bhava clearly indicates his devotion to
Visnite Cult. This devotion was undoubtedly the cause of conflict
against the last Buddhist King Rudravarman of Funan. The Mon
Tradition appeared to have a clear recollection of the legendary
king Tisa of Takkasila whom we had identified as Bhavavarman. A Mon
Chronicle recalls a severe set back of Buddhism under his reign.
There was a king of Thaton named Kalavantisa; a group of naga had
persuaded him to destroy Buddhism. All Buddha images had been thrown
in the river. There was a daughter of a wealthy family name Mith Tho
who was a devout Buddhist. Ignoring the king's order, she went on
saving the Buddha images and kept them in her house. Accusing of
violating the king order, she was sentenced to dead. However,
protected by her merit, the executor was not able to do his job, no
matter which way he tried. He failed to kill her either by burning
or put her in an elephant path. In frustration, the king asked her
if she knows of any magic. She replied that the only magic she knows
is her belief in Buddha's teaching. The king Tisa, recognizing his
mistake, converted himself to Buddhism and took Mith Tho as his
queen (*).
As we had seen (Chenla: LCM), Bhavavarman posthumous name was
Kalakantisapada that relates him to the Kala king Tisa of the story.
Devoted to Vinuite, he was misled by the nagas to wipe out Buddhism
from the Funan Kingdom. Just as the legend says, a devoted Buddhist
name Mit-tho saved him from the madness and after marrying her he
became a devote Buddhist himself. Many Khmer inscription mention
that his queen Kambojarajalaksmi was a relative of the Kambojan king
Shrestavarman royal line whose connection with Buddhism was well
known during the Funan Empire. Undoubtedly she was the heroine of
the Mon legend who sacrificed her life to save Buddhism. Her
commemoration as the "Laksmi of Kamboja king" tells us, one way or
another, about her important role in the reign of her king. With her
devotion, Buddhism had been reinstated and thrived at full force to
become the dominant religion of the Sri Vijaya Empire.
The restoration of Buddhism
On his first trip to India in 671, I-ching recalled his stop for six
month at Ligor (Sri Dhammaraja) to study Sanskrit grammar. His note
on the stay provides important information about Buddhism practiced
at the region.
In the fortified city of Fo-shih, there are more than a thousand of
Buddhist priests whose minds are bent on study and good works. They
examine and study all possible subjects exactly as in Madhyadesa;
their rules and ceremonies are identical with those in India.
Clearly the contrast of Vyadhapura where Buddhism was virtually
destroyed (Chenla: LCM), Sri Dhammaraja emerged as the new Buddhist
cultural center of Southeast Asia. The practices were very much in
tune with India, that I-ching recommended to his fellow Chinese
monks who were looking for more in sight of Buddhism to stop first
at Sri Dhammaraja.
If a Chinese priest wishes to go to the west to understand and read
the original Buddhist texts there, he would be wise to spend a year
or two in Fo-shih and practice the proper rules there; he might then
go on to central India.
On his return from India where he spent ten years at the University
of Nalanda, I-ching spent four more years at Fo-shih between 685 and
689. He had copied and translated Sanskrit Buddhist texts into
Chinese.
Malayu under the Sri Vijaya
The new history of the Tang mentions the first embassy of Ma-lo-yu
in 644-45. In 689, he had recruited four collaborators from his
brief trip to Canton, he returned to settle in Fo-shih. During his
last stay, he stayed two months in Mo-lo-yu in 671 and noted in his
record that Mo-lo-yu is now the country of Shih-li-fo-shih. In the
inscription of Ligor (Southeast Malaya), there are commemoration, in
each face, of two kings apparently with different dynasties. On its
face A, there is reference to a Srivijaya king Dhamasetu. He
dedicated in 775 AD a shrine to Buddha and two Buddhisatvas. On its
other face B, there is a reference to a king who bears the title of
Maharaja, but the date and the name of the king was missing.
Scholars speculated that the latter king could the Seilendra king
Dharanindra of the time. The fact that two kings of different
dynasties chose the same stone to inscribe their own commemoration
reflects the collaboration between them.
THE LAST STANDING OF THE CHENLA COURT
Without the backing of his uncle Bhavavarman, Isanavarman obviously
strengthen his alliance with Champapura and both courts had turned
to China for support. It was happening during the Tang Dynasty whose
diplomacy with the Saka world resulted in the high achievement of
commercial development with the west. Beside the full control of the
Silk route at Central Asia, the Tang dynasty was looking to control
the southern provinces and Chenla was there to be allied with
(Chiangmai, LCM).
Relationship with Champapura
An inscription found at Mi-son (BEFEO t.3: Stele de Sambhuvarman a
Mison, M. L. Finot), not far from that of Bhadravarman recounted on
one of its face an event during the reign of Sri Rudravarman. In the
saka year, determined only by the legible digit 4 as a year of the
fifth century, a fire destroyed the sanctuary of the god Bhadrasvara
built by the first Bhadravarman. The next king, with the coronation
name (Abhisekanama) of Sambhuvarman and with the religious
(dindikanama) name of Prasasthadhamma, managed to restore the
sanctuary and rededicate to the god Sambhubhadrasvara. The
inscription, moreover, is the first inscription to mention about
Champa Desa in relation with the new court of Sambhupura. The
mentioning about Champadesa reveals that he was related to the
previous Champa court and was trying to bring back the Cham legacy.
Sambhuvarman whose reign ended in 629, received a delegation from
Chenla, minister Simhadeva of king Mahendravarman. His son
Kandarpadhamma succeeded him and had a peaceful reign. He sent rich
presents to the emperor Tai Tsung of the Tang in 630 and 631. The
Cham tradition mentions about the union between the Cham king
Jagadharma and the princess Sarvani, a daughter of Isanavarman.
Continuing the policy of his father, Isanavarman obviously
maintained good relationship with the Saka world, a Saka Bramhmana
arriving from the Dekkan also married one of his daughters.
The attack of the Mans
Chinese texts mention that during the reign of Isanavarman, Chenla
had made alliance with Thsan Pan and Tchu-Krang, but fought with Lin-
yi and Tai-Yuan. It indicates that Lin-yi (Prey-nokor) was not part
of Champapura and along with Tai-Yuan fought alongside the Funan
kings against Chenla. Champapura, on the other hand, had been in
good relationship with the Chenla Empire since the reign of
Mahendravarman. It appears that the Funan kings had managed to
capture back Prey-nokor and at the same time were helped by rulers
of Nan-chao whom the Chinese referred as the Man rebels. A Chinese
text recorded one of their attacks over the last of the Chenla
empire.
The Man rebels once led an army of cavalry as far as the seashore.
When they saw the green waves roaring and breaking, they felt
disappointed and took their army and went back home.
Another Chinese text (JA 46 (Nov. 1958): The early Siam in Burma's
History, G.H. Luce.) describes their attack against the city of Nu-
wang.
The City was Nu-wang and was located at over 30 day-stages distant
from Jin-Nan Chieh-tu on the Man and is 10 day-stages distant from
Huan-Chou. They regularly carried on trade with the common people of
Huan-Chou. The Man rebels once led 2000 men to attack the kingdom.
They were shot down by Nu-Wang with poisoned arrows. Not one in ten
survived. The Man rebels then retreated.
According to the Khmer chronicle, the Cham kings were loosing ground
and were forced to retreat. They had to reposition themselves to the
South.
He (Prah Thong) then strikes back to chase out the Cham kings; some
escaped to Bayangkau, some to the mount Isvara.
The story is checked-out by score of vestiges left behind by king
Isanavarman at the realm of the southern territory that includes a
stone temple built on mount Bayangkau. Another Chinese text (JA 46
(Nov. 1958): The early Siam in Burma's History, G.H. Luce.)
describes similar attack by the Man rebels, this time to the ancient
Khmer (Kun-lun) territory where Isanavarman was taking refuge.
Kun-lun kingdom is 81 day-stages from the Man boarders (JA 46 (Nov.
1958): The early Siam in Burma's History, G.H. Luce.) Products of
the land are the dark wood perfume, sandalwood perfume, dark-red
sandalwood perfume, betel-nut trees, glazed ware, rock crystal,
bottle gourds, unburned brick, etc..., various perfumes and herbs,
precious stones, rhinoceros, etc. Once the Man rebels led an army
with cavalry to attack it. The (people of) Kun-lun kingdom left the
road open and let them advance. Then they cut the road behind the
army and connected it with the river, letting the water cover it.
Whether they advanced and retreated, (the Man) were helpless. Over
ten thousands died of hunger. Of those who did not die, the Kun-lun
severed the right wrists and let them go home.
The passage indicates that the Khmer (Kun-lun) kingdom, obviously
under the Chenla kings, was fighting against the Man rebels forcing
them to withdraw after severe loss. But that was just another
setback, Chenla along with Champapura was gradually loosing ground
and finally were forced off the mainland.
The last records
The inscription of Ang Chumnik ends its genealogy of the Chenla
kings with Jayavarman I (657-681) who is mentioned as the son of
Isanavarman. Found at Ba Phnom, the inscription was inscribed by
members of the Chenla court and provided good information about the
end of the Chenla Empire. The inscription indicates that Jayavarman
I left the court to his uncle of mother side who took care of the
family's fortune.
Next the king (Jayavarman I), with all marks of honor, left (the
court) to brother of his mother, who without having the title,
enjoyed the fortune of a king.
The inscription does not provide any more information about him but
along with other inscriptions give however whimsy picture of the
Chenla court at Ba Phnom. One inscription left by his daughter,
queen Jayadevi mentioned donations to a sanctuary of Siva
Tripurantaka and complained of the misfortunes of the times. This
sanctuary was founded by the princess Sophajaya, also a daughter of
Jayavarman I who married the Sivaite Brahman Sakravarmin born in
India. The unfortunate circumstances mentioned in many of the
inscriptions, could be a prelude to the end of the Chenla court. The
inscription of Ang Chunik mentions next the formation of the city
Adhyapura under the government of Jayavarman's uncle.
After the rule of succession of the family, the king by recognizing
that he was the righteous man commissioned him with honor to take
care the government of the city of Adhyapura. Then only under his
ruling with justice that procured the non-stop prosperity, the city
of Adhyapura had lived up to its name.
That was presumably the fate of the Chenla court; under the control
of the new Khmer Empire, it was reduced back to its beginning, a
wealthy society holding no status of kingship.
Adhyapura
We could identify without much doubt that Adhyapura was at the site
of Ba Phnom. During the breakup of the Funan Empire, it was known as
the Kun Lun Kingdom in Chinese text and was the seat of the last
Chenla Empire. It was at this place that I-ching mentioned about the
fate of the Buddhism religion during the Chenla attack.
Setting out south-westwards, one reaches within a month, Poh-nan
(Kuo), formaly called Fu-nan. Of old it was a country, the
inhabitants of which lived naked; the people were mostly worshippers
of heaven. Later on, Buddhism flourished there, but a wicked king
has now expelled and exterminated them all, and there are no members
of the Buddhist Brotherhood at all, while adherents of other
religions live intermingled.
Poh-nan is obviously I-ching's reference to Ba Phnom which was
previously the capital of the Funan Empire subjugated by the Chenla
Empire.
Lingaparvata
An inscription found at Vat Phu (BEFEO II: Stele de Vat Phu, M. A.
Barth) witnesses the presence of Jayavarman I at the location
mentioned in the inscription as Lingaparvata, on the Khorat Plateau
far away north from Ba-phnom.
The master of masters of the land, Sri Jayavarman, issues his
command (as followed): At this Srimate Lingaparvata, all the living
of this place would not be harmed by nobody, even if they had
committed sins. What offering to God here, or others would stay as
his.
This is the first inscription that refers Wat Phu as Lingaparvata.
To the Chenla court, Lingaparvatava was the sacred place for the
Chenla King to perform the annual human sacrifice (Chenla, LCM). The
inscription implicates that the sacrifice was still carried on
during the reign of Jayavarman I whose reign marked the decline of
the Chenla Empire. The passage seams to indicate that the goal of
the sacrifice was to be protected by God from any harm, regardless
of the sins they have committed. During the last of his reign,
Jayavarman I had to abandon Ba-phnom and moved the Chenla court up
north to Wat Phu. It was obviously a strategic move; closer to the
Chinese border, he could find protection from the Tang Dynasty
(Chiangmai, LCM). At the same time, evidences show that the Chenla
hard core with the legacy of the late king Isanavarman was already
regrouping in Central Java where they found support from the
emerging Chola Empire of South India.
THE RISE OF THE JAVANESE EMPIRE
Mataram had been cited in Javanese legend to receive the Saka
immigrants from Gujarat since the (Sakadvipa, LCM) the antiquity
time. Under the control of sri Vijaya, the Saka communities in Java
appeared to stay in the background. After the fall of Chenla, the
situation was going to change as the New History of the Tang already
cited the emergence of a kingdom named Ho-ling in Central Java.
Ho-ling at Central Java
The New History of the Tang has many passages relating to the
Embassies of Ho-ling; the first three embassies were dated 640, 648
and 666. The Chinese passage recounts the story of a queen named
Sima, apparently ruling over Ho-ling in 674.
The ruler of Ta-tche sent a bag of gold to be laid down within her
frontiers. The people, walking by, avoided the bag and it was
untouched for a long time. One day, one of the crown princes stepped
over it and the queen was furious. She ordered to have her own son
executed, but with the objection of her court, a compromise was set
to just cut off the toe that touch the bag of the gold.
The story obviously portrays a test of willpower orchestrated by the
kingdom of Ta-tche (the great kingdom) which is no other than the
Sri Vijaya Empire. As a new established state next to the already
powerful and rival Sri Vijaya, Ho-ling was not obviously welcome and
harassement continued. Through her determination, continues the
Chinese text, the queen Sima won respect from Ta-tche and we shall
see that her tenacity leaded to the formation of the Sanjaya Empire,
set to become a new maritime power of the South China sea. About the
origin of Ho-ling, we need to look back to what happened during the
fall of the Chenla Empire. The Chinese word "Ho-ling" is translated
into the river of reed, a reference to the province "Stung-treng" of
Cambodia. Evidence show that during its last stand at the mainland,
the Land Chenla relocated it capital to Stung-treng (Chiangmai,LCM).
The Javanese court might introduce itself the first time to the Tang
Dynasty as the Chenla court of Ho-ling. The reference however lasted
only a short while as the Chenla court of Ho-ling was about to
succumb and never been mentioned again in the next Chinese records.
Cho-pa or She-po, a reference to Champa, was also a reference of the
Javanese court in Chinese texts. These facts constituted evidence
that supports the connection between the displaced Chenla and Champa
courts with the formation of the new Javanese Empire. Another
supporting evidence to the same conclusion is the legacy of the late
Chenla king Isanavarman; Javanese inscriptions later attest the
reverence of IsanaBhadrasvara, the god king of Iasanavarman, as the
divine protector of the Javanese Empire.
Sanjaya
A Sanskrit inscription found in the central part of the Island of
Java among the ruins of the Sivaite sanctuary of Changal, was an
attribution to the king Sanjaya. The inscription mentions the
erection of a linga on the island of Yava. Described to be rich in
grain and gold mines, the country of Kunjarakunja appeared to be
located between Travancore and Tinnevelly, the site of the sanctuary
dedicated to the sage Agastya. In Indian Tradition, the sage Agastya
was credited to conquer the natural barrier between the
Transgangetic India and the south, and in the process bring along
the new development of the Sivaite culture from the north to the
South. As the sea communication between Java and the South Indian
continent was very much established since the antiquity time, this
South Indian cultural evolution obviously reached the Java Island.
Sanjaya was obviously patronizing this cultural transfer and was
capitalizing on it. The same inscription refers two royal figures of
the name Sanna and Sannaha as predecessors of Sanjaya. According to
Javanese Tradition (History of Indonesia, B. R. Chatterji), Sanna
and Sannaha were brother and sister, Sanjaya was the son of Sannaha.
Looking back into the Chenla legacies (The split of Chenla, LCM),
the daughter of Jayavarman I, queen Jayadevi mentioned in one of her
inscriptions about donations to a sanctuary of Siva Tripurantaka and
complained of the misfortunes of the times. This sanctuary was
founded by the princess Sophajaya, also a daughter of Jayavarman I
who married the Sivaite Brahman Sakravarmin born in India. It is in
high probability that this Sivaite Brahman was connected to the sage
Agastya who became to be known as the Sivaite Guru in South India.
This new development that was credited to the sage Agastya in
spreading the first time of Sivaite culture in South India was
strongly seen as contributing later to the emergence of the Chola
Empire. On the other hand, Sanjaya might have been related to the
queen Jayadevi who was portrayed as queen Sima in the Chinese story.
After their settlement, evidence show that the new Javanese rulers
did not forget theirs past glory over the mainland during the Chenla
era. Inscriptions of both Java and Champapura described the naval
attacks orchestrated by the Javanese Empire making its suzerainty
felt along the eastern coast of Indochinese Peninsula. This campaign
was obviously conducted in retaliation to the insult of the Khmer
prince, recounted in the Arab story (Dvaravati:The Cradle of the
Angkorian Empire, LCM).
Tribhuvanesvara
The inscription of Veal Kantel (Le cambodge:La region de Bassac,
Aymonier) commemorates the erection of the god king Tribhuvanesvara,
by the Brahman Somasarman, husband of a daughter of king Viravarman
and sister of Bhavavarman. The consecration was followed by the
usual presents to the God King, among them a complete set of
Mahabhatara, the Ramayana and the Purana. We shall see later
(Ramanadesa, LCM) that Tribhuvanaditya which is the visnuite version
of the sivaite Tribhuvanesvara, emerged later in the court of the
Talaing king Makutavamsa of Tathon and was retained in the court of
Ramandesa ever since. On the other hand, Tribhuvanesvara also
emerged in the Angkorian court during the high of the Chola
influence in Southeast Asia. Obviously Tribhuvanaditya was part of
the new Chola development in India that mirrored the global rise of
the Saka clan.
The Chola Connection
The connection between the South Indian Hindu religious development
with the rise of the Chenla Empire was obvious. Inscriptions witness
the marriage of Sakabhramanas from South India and princesses of
both the Chenla and Champa courts. The word "Chola" itself seems to
indicate that the connection was of Southeast Asian instead of
Indian origin. Since the very beginning, Chinese texts consistently
referred the leadership of Lin-yi as Chu-lien. From: there, we had
identified its connection with the Choladhara legacy of Prey Nokor
(Nagdvipa, LCM). The emergence of the Chola Empire of South India
was not by all means a coincident; it was the resuscitation of the
ancient Kamara legacy, this time under the Saka global leadership.
Fairly enough, the Chinese texts still used the word "Chu-lien" to
refer to the new Empire. For long, scholars had been puzzed about
Chinese texts mentioning about this Arianized dynasty being absorb
back into the Southeast Asian Sri Vijaya Empire, five centuries
later. Our evidence might help explain the enigma; the Arianized
world was transient and was a global development. In the Javanese
Ramayana (The Diversion: The exploit of Rama, LCM), the elder
brother Rama was depicted as a darker skin Saka while the younger
brother Lakshana was depicted as the yellow skin Kamara. This
brotherhood formed the Chola Empire and was fighting against the
Buddhist State of Sri Langka. During the fighting, Lakshana was
portrayed to be withdrawn and reluctant to fight against Ravana, the
ruler of Sri Langka. The epic ends with the fall of Ravana and the
reunification back between Rama and Sita. If the epic does not stop
there and is allowed to continues we shall see that Ravana will be
resuscitate and this time Rama would be in trouble as his brother
Lakshana would join the Ravana clan in the end.
