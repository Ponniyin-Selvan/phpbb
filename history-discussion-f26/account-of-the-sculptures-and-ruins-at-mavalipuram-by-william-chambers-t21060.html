Dear Friends,

I got a chance to find one of the oldest (may be rare
collection) book from British Library converted in e-Book format.

The book named "DissertationsAndMiscellaneousPieces Â–Vol I" ,this
was written by group of people on 1784(not sure the exact
publication year),published in London Printed by G.Nicols ,Book
Seller to his Majesty.

One of the very interesting chapters on this book titled as

"Miscellaneous Pieces Relating to the HISTORY and ANTIQUITIES, THE
ARTS SCIENCE and LITERATURE OF ASIA. Account of the
Sculptures and Ruins at Mavalipuram By William Chambers,ESQ at 17th
June 1784"

I have attached the PDF file of this Chapter in the file section.

With the great interest I read that chapter which induced lot of
interest on some of his observations. The "Mavalipuram", which he
means is our Great Mahabalipuram.Here are the interesting
observations from the book,

1. In the initial pages he took great pain to arrive at the
Mavalipuram by comparing Sanskrit and by accounting the Mahabarat
story.

2. In his entire chapter he refers the native language as "Tamulic"
instead of Tamil.

3.The very most interesting observation was, he says, that he heard
from native community that the native's oldest people used to say
that they see a copper plated temple top in the sea which glitters
when the sun ray falls on it."

Assuming that the oldest person of 1784 was 100 years of
old at his/her younger age, if he had seen the copper plated temple
top on the sea, then it should be some where in 1690-1700 AD.

That means on 16th century people could have seen some more temples
which we did not see so far in Mahabalipuram.

4. Also on the writer period the "Tamil Â– Brahmi" inscription found
on the "Rajasimha" sculptures were not known. The author was trying
to compare that inscription with the language from Bali.

5. Also the current shore temple did not mention in any of his
descriptions.

I would request the expert group in our forum spend some time on

original author in the book.

Some more interesting facts :




It is widely believed by the historians that




"The myths of Mahabalipuram were written down by British traveler J.
Goldingham, who visited the town in 1798, at which time it was known to
sailors as the Seven Pagodas. Legend had it that six temples were
submerged beneath the waves, with the seventh temple still standing on
the seashore"




Refer these articles




http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/south_asia/1923794.stm


http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/south_asia/4312024.stm


http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/south_asia/4257181.stm


http://india.krishna.org/Articles/2002/10/006.html





But it doesn't look like true, William Chambers had written about this
in his writings,he says,

"The rock,or rather hill of stone,on which great part of these works are
executed,is one of the principal marks for mariners as they approach the
coast,and to them the place is known by the name of the Seven
Pagodas,possibly because the summits of the rock have presented them
with that idea as passed:"




and this was acknowledged by Graham Hancock in his book "Underworld :
The Mysterious Origins of Civilisation".See here

http://www.grahamhancock.com/features/m ... ods-p5.htm

Website.

myth.In fact Graham Hancock was part of the expedition team along with
NIO(National Institute of oceanography. refer the first article) to
found the underwater ruins of remaining submerged structures.

http://www.india-atlantis.org/




The real interesting fact is that both Goldnigham as well as William
Champers both were accounting in their writings about the stories from
our older generations on the submerged temples

Another interesting question is, William Champers writes about the Lion
Structures in two places.

In the first place he describes as

"On ascending the hill by its slope on the north a very singular piece
of sculpture presents itself to view.On a plain surface of the rock
which may have served as the floor of some apartment,there is a platform
of stone,about eight or nine feet long,by three or four wide,in a
situation rather elevated with two or three steps leading up to
it,perfectly resembling a couch or bed,and a lion very well executed at
the upper end of it by way of pillow,the whole of one piece,being part
of the hill itself"




This cannot be this one here in the link

Because he explains the above as

"Near these also stand an elephant full as big as life,and a lion much

of one stone"

recent one found after tsunami,See the article on BBC,
