Hi venkat

what you say is true about singapore. The malayan tiger was very much
roaming in singapore till 1930 ( there is a story that the last wild
tiger was shot in the bar of the famous raffles hotel.....)

On the larger question - if you study the habitats of the lions and
the tigers - you will spot the reasons - tigers prefer dense forest -
where their stripes make them blend in with the folliage. whereas
lions are built to survive by merging with dry shrubs etc - thats one
of the reasons you see lions in africa but no tigers.

in that line of thought, south india has both these habitats. i have
few old ( british books on hunting big game ) - and south india is
more for leapards than tigers/lions. so we need to go further back -
going right back to pallava times - we could draw a parallel from the
forest scene depicted in the arjuna penance/bagiratha panel ( quoting
from mr swaminathan -pudukottai work on mallai - yet to be published)

"
There are fifteen lions and other large cats and two little cubs, ten
deer and antelopes, mountain goats, four monkeys, a hare, an iguana
stalking an unsuspecting bird, one boar, a tortoise, two jungle cocks
and two hens, and geese, a fair representation of the animal world. A
dynamic portraiture is a tiger suckling her two cubs, and similar
liveliness can be seen in the boar looking furtively behind for
enemies. Perhaps the best is the relaxed deer pair one of which
scratching its mouth with the paw, a classic piece. "

Taking artistic liberty - they would have obviously overplayed the
pallava lion banner - as is evident from the ratio of the prey species
in this scene - its however evident that lions were pretty much there
alonside other species in south india.

Further into chola land - we have the famous chevur battle
inscriptions of aditya - where even as a boy - he is said to have
toyed with the pandya forces - like a lion club sporting with an
elephant....lions are the only animals known to take on elephants and
that the inscriber knew of this only goes to show that both were co
existing.

But keeping in mind the number of large elephants in the chola army -
famous chinese traveller accounts that the chola government owns sixty
thousand war elephants, every one seven or eight feet high ) there
must be abundant forests - with vegetation enough to sustain such a
large wild population of elephants to provide continous feed to the
domesticated stock - it would have been ideal ground for tigers but
not for lions. so somewhere in old pallava territory during the 7th C
would be lion land - moving into chola country would be the land of
the tigers. tigers would have roamed over much of chera country as
well.
