sometime back madan's observation in anandavikadan that a exxagerated
history of tamil nadu was written by poets intoxicated on liquor
caused a riot in our group and out side.
today while re reading PS i found kalki having made the same
observation (almost the same words) in chapter 54 of book 1 where
aditya laments poetry is the only source of tamil history. and even
that is questionable.

how we missed this during those days of heated discussions i wonder.

To add to your observation,
in the chapter where Vandhiathevan meets Azhagiya cholar,
one of the poets gathered there sings a song which states that the chola
chakravarthy has given pari to surya and kari to indra and so on,
and the vendhan says that it was a joke that he composed for kundavai
when she was a kid, and how it has spread to the world and how the
future tamil world would make fun of him for writing such a poem.

the point is, there could have been a fair BIT of exageration in the
written literature.. however since there was quite a BIT of reality
undocumented this can be compensating..
it is just my view.. not to add fuel to the fire.

by sampath_srinivasaraghavan on Wed Feb 27, 2008 3:00 pm

--- Inponniyinselvan@yahoogroups.com, "R.venketesh"
wrote:


That is the prerogative of a novelist; he can follow any rule - 80/20
(history/fiction) or 20/80 or anything in between. He is honest in the
sense that he admits it.

But a historian is a person with a bias formed out of his early studies;
he will judiciously ignore events and evidences that reduce the
crdibility of his stand.

Even experimental scientists discard observations which are out of place
in the deduced scheme. Genes/free radicals/food/radiation etc which are
responsible for cancer/heart attack/liver-kidney damage change their
benign-malignant status too often for comfort.

Theoretical scientists start with a small set of postulates nad build on
them. The whole edifice rests on the 'unprovability' of the postulates.

In my PUC university exam, in Logic, this question was asked.

"All brahmins must be vegetarians because I have not come across any
brahmin who eats meat."

According to Aristotlean logic the answer is "Hasty generalization"
(concluded in haste). The answer "non-observation" is also a close
second, as the observed cases that do not fit the perceived "image" will
be quitely discarded by the 'logician'.

But as per our system, the data provided is insufficient to come to any
universal conclusion - the credentials of both the 'seeker' (who
concludes) and the 'sought' (brahmin vegetarians) must be known. We know
that bengali, kashmiri, oriya and saraswat brahmins, priestly class
included, eat no-vag food.

To cut a long story short - Kalki is a novelist while Madan is a
historian (not a history fiction writer). While Kalki may present
contrdictory views, Madan will not, but will dig his heels and find more
'evidences' to support his view.

Sampath
